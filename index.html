Nikola Tesla

(1856-1943)

“Peace can only come as a natural consequence of universal enlightenment.”

Nikola Tesla was a visionary inventor, electrical engineer, and physicist whose groundbreaking work in electromagnetism and wireless technology transformed the world. Born in 1856 in what is now Croatia, Tesla's innovations, including alternating current (AC) power, the Tesla coil, and advancements in radio, paved the way for modern electricity and telecommunications. Despite facing financial difficulties and often working in isolation, his contributions laid the foundation for much of the technology we take for granted today. This tribute page honors his genius, relentless pursuit of knowledge, and enduring legacy that continues to inspire innovation and progress.

Timeline highlighting key events in Nikola Tesla's life and career.

1856.

July 10: Nikola Tesla is born in Smiljan, Austrian Empire (now Croatia), to Georg and Georgina Tesla.

1875.

Enters the Technical University of Graz, Austria, to study electrical engineering but later leaves due to personal and financial struggles.

1879.

Moves to Budapest, Hungary, and works for the Central Telephone Exchange.

1881.

Moves to Paris and works for the Continental Edison Company.

1884.

Arrives in New York City with little more than a letter of recommendation for Thomas Edison.

Begins working for Thomas Edison on direct current (DC) systems.

1885.

Quits Edison’s company after disagreements over pay and the superiority of AC vs. DC power.

Secures funding to form his own company.

1887.

Invents the induction motor and makes major improvements to the alternating current (AC) system.

Marries science and industry, forging partnerships with industrial giants like George Westinghouse.

1888.

Demonstrates the AC motor and becomes a key figure in the "War of Currents", advocating for alternating current over Edison’s direct current.

Wins patent rights to the AC motor and system.

AC power transmission is chosen for the Niagara Falls power station, one of Tesla’s major triumphs.

1891.

Invents the Tesla Coil, used for high-frequency and high-voltage experiments.

Becomes a naturalized U.S. citizen.

1893.

Demonstrates wireless transmission of energy at the World’s Columbian Exposition in Chicago.

AC power is used to light the Exposition, further proving the viability of Tesla’s system.

1895.

Opens the Tesla Electric Light and Manufacturing company and develops wireless power transmission concepts.

1899.

Moves to Colorado Springs to conduct high-voltage experiments, including the famous Colorado Springs experiments on wireless transmission of electricity and lightning generation.

1901.

Begins construction of the Wardenclyffe Tower in Shoreham, New York, a facility for wireless transmission of energy.

The project faces financial issues, and construction is halted in 1906.

1904.

Tesla is granted over 300 patents by this time, spanning a wide range of technologies, including early work on radio waves and remote control.

1915.

Tesla writes a letter to Time Magazine emphasizing his belief that technology, especially wireless transmission, could bring about a "universal system" of global communication.

1917.

The U.S. Navy adopts Tesla's ideas for radio-controlled torpedoes.

1920s-1930s.

Tesla's health and financial stability begin to decline.

Despite his major innovations, he struggles to gain funding for his visionary projects, including the wireless transmission of energy worldwide.

1943.

January 7: Nikola Tesla dies penniless in New York City at the age of 86.

Posthumous Recognition.

Tesla’s work and inventions gain widespread recognition and acclaim after his death.

The Tesla Coil, alternating current, and wireless transmission technologies are regarded as foundational to modern electrical engineering.

In 1960, the International System of Units adopts the unit of magnetic flux density, the Tesla (T), in his honor.

Tesla's visionary concepts continue to influence modern science, technology, and popular culture today.
